What Is an eSIM and How Does It Work?

An eSIM (embedded SIM) is a digital SIM card built directly into your smartphone, tablet, or smartwatch. Unlike a traditional plastic SIM card, an eSIM can be programmed remotely and allows you to switch between mobile carriers without physically swapping cards. This technology, standardized by the GSMA, is now supported by over 400 devices including all iPhones from iPhone XR onwards, Samsung Galaxy S21 and newer, and most Google Pixel phones.

When you travel internationally, an eSIM lets you purchase and activate a local data plan within minutes — without visiting a store, waiting for delivery, or dealing with tiny plastic cards. You simply scan a QR code or use an app, and your eSIM is ready to use.

Benefits of Using an eSIM for Travel

  • Instant activation: Purchase and activate a plan before you leave home, or even while on the plane.
  • No roaming fees: Local eSIM plans are significantly cheaper than international roaming charges from your home carrier.
  • Keep your number: Use your original SIM for calls and texts while your eSIM handles data — perfect for receiving 2FA codes.
  • Eco-friendly: No plastic waste from disposable SIM cards.
  • Multi-country plans: Regional eSIM plans cover entire continents like Europe or Southeast Asia under one plan.

How to Choose the Best eSIM Plan

Selecting the right eSIM plan depends on several factors: how long you'll be traveling, how much data you typically use, and whether you need voice calls or just data. For most leisure travelers, a data-only eSIM is sufficient since most calls and messages can be handled via WhatsApp, iMessage, or other apps over WiFi.

At eSIMly, we recommend comparing plans based on price per GB, network coverage quality, plan validity duration, and provider reputation. Our comparison tool lets you filter all these variables instantly across 50+ providers.

eSIM vs Physical SIM Card: Which Is Better?

For travelers, eSIMs win on almost every dimension. They're faster to set up, more flexible, and often cheaper since providers have lower distribution costs. The only scenario where a physical SIM might be preferred is if your device doesn't support eSIM — though this is becoming increasingly rare in modern smartphones.
